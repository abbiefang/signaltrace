# SignalTrace — User Personas

Three distinct users. Different life stages, different pain points, different relationships with their own judgment. What they share: intelligence, high standards, and a recurring experience of knowing something is off before they can prove it.

---

## Persona 1 — Lena, 27 — The One Who Keeps Giving Benefit of the Doubt

**Location:** New York. Works in brand strategy at an agency. Busy, socially fluent, high output.

**Dating context:** Has been actively dating on Hinge and Bumble for 18 months. Talks to 2-4 people at a time, usually has 1-2 she's more serious about. Not a casual dater — she's looking for something real, but she's not in a rush. She has healthy standards on paper.

**The problem:** She keeps getting to week 4-6 with someone and finding herself unable to make a call. She knows something is slightly off — the plans are always vague, or the compliments are high but the follow-through is low, or he's warm in text but oddly distracted in person. She notices it. But then something nice happens, and she decides she was probably overthinking. She has smart, perceptive friends, but they can only hear so much before they say "honestly he sounds fine, you're just scared." So she stays in it longer than she should, and then when things end she looks back and realizes the signals were there from week 2.

**Core pain point:** She has the data but she keeps discounting it. She's not missing the signals — she's overriding them, one interaction at a time.

**What she tells herself:** "I don't want to be the girl who writes someone off because of one weird text."

**What's actually happening:** She's applying individual-event logic to a behavioral pattern. She evaluates each signal in isolation. When you do that, every red flag looks manageable. It's only when you stack them sequentially that the pattern becomes undeniable.

**What triggers her to try SignalTrace:**
A specific moment — she's thinking about James, trying to remember if he's ever made a concrete plan without her initiating. She realizes she can't remember. She starts scrolling back through iMessage to check. It takes 25 minutes. She finds what she suspected: he's proposed three "soon"s and zero specific dates in three weeks. She felt something like relief and dread at the same time. She wishes she'd had somewhere to put this sooner.

**What she tracks in the app:**
- Statements he's made vs. follow-through behavior (did the plan actually happen)
- Who initiated what, in what order, over time
- Her debrief after each date — what felt good, what felt slightly off
- Conflicting signals (warm texts, passive in-person)
- Her own emotional temperature — is she more anxious after each date, or steadier?

**What she wants out of it:**
She doesn't want the app to tell her what to do. She wants the app to hold the record so she can't gaslight herself. She wants to be able to look at a clean summary and say: yes, this is a pattern, I saw it, I'm making a clear-eyed call. She wants to leave without second-guessing herself for three weeks afterward.

**The feature she'd use most:**
Conflicting Signal Detection and the Statement Index. She needs the contradiction surfaced explicitly — she keeps experiencing it emotionally but can't hold the full picture in her head at once.

**How she'd describe the app to a friend:**
"It's basically a second brain for the part of dating where you already know something but haven't admitted it yet."

## Persona 2 — Margot, 34 — The One Who Doesn't Trust Her Own Judgment Anymore

**Location:** London, or a major city equivalent. Works in UX research or clinical psychology adjacent field — she's trained to observe people carefully, which makes it worse.

**Dating context:** Was in a five-year relationship that ended 14 months ago. The relationship was not abusive — it just slowly became a place where her standards got quietly compressed. She stayed two years too long. She's been dating again for about six months; it's going okay on the surface. She's matching with solid people. But every time she starts to like someone, a part of her pulls back hard — because the last time she trusted herself, she didn't catch it in time.

**The problem:** Her judgment antenna works fine. Her willingness to trust it is broken. She keeps second-guessing herself not because she's naive but because she has proof that she can be wrong. She spent years rationalizing things in her last relationship, and now she can't tell the difference between healthy caution and trauma-informed paralysis.

**Core pain point:** She needs external structure to re-establish trust in her own perception. Not validation — she's smart enough to see through hollow encouragement. She needs a system that lets her accumulate evidence over time and then look at it neutrally, the way she would if she were doing research on someone else.

**What she tells herself:** "I just need more information before I make any decisions." (This is partly true and partly avoidance — she uses it interchangeably and can't always tell which is which.)

**What's actually happening:** She's operating in a kind of epistemic fog where she has sharp observations but no trusted framework for weighing them. Every signal she gets, she finds a counterargument for. This isn't stupidity — it's a defensive pattern built from a long period of ignoring her own read.

**What triggers her to try SignalTrace:**
She's seeing someone who is, by most measures, good. He texts reliably. He makes plans. He's kind. But she has a low-level unease she can't justify, and she doesn't know if it's real or residual. She tells her therapist. Her therapist says: "What evidence do you have?" She realizes she doesn't have evidence — she has impressions. She wants a place to actually put the evidence so she can stop living entirely in her head.

**What she tracks in the app:**
- Specific things he says about his intentions and values — she wants to see them in writing over time
- How her anxiety fluctuates date by date — is it decreasing (which is good) or staying flat (which tells her something)
- Whether she's setting pre-mortem exit criteria and actually meaning them, or setting them and immediately softening them
- The pace of emotional disclosure — is depth increasing at a healthy rate, or is it stalling?
- Her own emotional patterns across this person vs. her last relationship — any pattern overlap

**What she wants out of it:**
Confidence that her reads are real. Not reassurance — just the ability to say "I tracked this, I saw this, this is what actually happened" without her memory warping it retroactively. She also, honestly, wants to know if she's repeating anything. She doesn't think she is. But she wants the evidence that she isn't.

**The feature she'd use most:**
Pre-Mortem Exit Criteria (she needs to commit to her standards while she's clear-headed), the Decision Journal (she wants a record that future-her can reference), and the About Me patterns section (she wants to know if she's seeing recurring dynamics across people she dates).

**How she'd describe the app to a friend:**
"It's like a research log for something you can't be objective about but desperately need to be."

## Persona 3 — Celine, 31 — The One Who Keeps Picking the Same Structural Pattern

**Location:** Toronto or Berlin. Works in finance, law, or a demanding creative field with a very clear hierarchy. High-functioning. Has her life together visibly.

**Dating context:** Has dated more extensively than average — she's been on 50+ first dates in the last three years and has had four situationships or short relationships that all ended similarly. She's not inexperienced. She's actually very good at early dating — she's sharp, interesting, calibrated. The problem always shows up around month 2 or 3, when the structural dynamic becomes clear.

**The problem:** She keeps ending up in the same dynamic: someone who is emotionally compelling and high-stimulation in early stages, but who doesn't actually commit — not because they're bad people, but because they're avoidant, or non-committal, or just not in the same place. She knows this intellectually. She's talked about it in therapy. She can narrate the pattern fluently. But she can't seem to exit it in real time — because each person feels like a legitimate exception to the rule, in the beginning.

**Core pain point:** She has cross-relationship pattern recognition in hindsight, but not in the moment. Each situation feels new. She needs a tool that gives her a real-time view of whether this person is tracking differently from the last three, or whether the first three months look exactly the same.

**What she tells herself:** "He's actually different. He's direct. He said what he wants early."

**What's actually happening:** She's confusing communication style (confident, direct verbal expression) with behavioral follow-through (consistent, advancing action). The men she's drawn to are often good at articulating what they want; they're just not reliable at delivering it. She mistakes articulacy for commitment.

**What triggers her to try SignalTrace:**
She ends her most recent situationship — six weeks, ended exactly the way the last one did, with a vague fade after things started to deepen. She texts her friend: "This keeps happening and I don't understand why." Her friend says: "You literally said the same thing about Marcus." She thinks: "If this keeps happening, there has to be a pattern I'm not seeing." She starts looking for something that can help her track across relationships, not just within one.

**What she tracks in the app:**
- Across multiple people: how long before her enthusiasm peaked, how long before momentum stalled
- Behavioral consistency specifically around planning, follow-through, and emotional availability (the three dimensions she keeps getting wrong)
- Whether she sets exit criteria and actually enforces them, or allows the deadline to quietly pass
- The Dating Energy Budget — she has a tendency to add people when anxious about a current person, which dilutes her judgment
- Her "high" period vs. "unease" period per person — she wants to see if the pattern is she gets the most excited right before things fall apart

**What she wants out of it:**
A real-time mirror. Not to tell her what to do — she has strong opinions — but to show her whether this situation is tracking differently from the last three or whether the first 30 days look identical. She wants to catch the repeat before she's already six weeks in. She also wants to know her own pattern: does her excitement level actually correlate with outcome? She suspects the inverse is true. She wants the data.

**The feature she'd use most:**
The cross-person pattern view in About Me (especially the Emotional Patterns and Growth Timeline), Behavioral Prediction (she wants to know if the app's prediction for this person matches what she remembers predicting for the last one), and the Dating Fatigue Detection (she uses a full pipeline to avoid anxiety, and she knows this makes her judgment worse — she needs the app to call it).

**How she'd describe the app to a friend:**
"It's the only tool that actually shows you if you're running the same script again. In the moment it never feels like a repeat. This makes it undeniable."

## Shared Behavior Across All Three

Despite their differences, all three users share several traits that define the core SignalTrace user:

They are observant. They notice things before they can articulate them. The app doesn't give them better perception — it gives their perception somewhere to land and be held.

They are already doing informal versions of this. Lena scrolls iMessage looking for patterns. Margot keeps a notes app journal. Celine has a mental model of the last four situationships that she runs comparisons against. SignalTrace formalizes what they're already trying to do in scraps.

They don't want the app to make decisions for them. They want it to remove the distortion — of memory, of hope, of isolation from their own earlier judgment — so they can make the decision themselves, cleanly.

They are done with the loop they're in. Not dramatic about it. Just clear that something about their current approach isn't working, and ready for a tool that treats them like someone capable of handling accurate information.
